Please excuse me while I just ramble ... This is just going to read like a journal and I don't have time to re-write/organize the information. 

I believe that I have an ideal location for a studio: under my garage. The previous owner of our home put in stairs, exterior double solid-core doors, connected it to the home's alarm system, and turned the space into a 26' by 22' woodshop. 

We live on a hill, so the garage floor is supported on big glue-laminated beams and 2" by 12" joists on 8" centers. The floor has two inches of light weight cement poured over 3/4" plywood.  Additionally, half of the room has some cement walls on three sides and is partially underground due to the location on the hill.

The space was divided by a wall which we demolished, as you can see in the pictures above.  Half of the room was a non-standard height, so we dug down and set up to pour a footing. We lowered the floor by 20". 

The pump truck arrived and we did the pour. 

What a mess! :-) You can't see it in the picture, but the new footing has a water channel molded into the top that is about 1" deep and 3" wide that runs the entire perimeter. Should any water seep/leak into the area, it will have a clear path to the crawlspace below that does not include my new floor. 

Then the forms came off, we stuck in a vapor barrier, and started hanging floor joists. The live/tracking room above the new floor will be a room within a room design with a floating ceiling.

Note that the new floor is isolated from the existing floor. This is by design to isolate the control room from the live/tracking room.   We got a chance to test hits theory out when the air compressor for the framing nail gun kicked in while resting on one of the floors.  The floor the compressor was resting on vibrated like crazy, but the other floor wasn't moving at all; cool! 

My little helper and I strung nylon line under the joists and put 9" of insulation in the floor.

I routed conduit for audio cables and Romex for the in-floor power receptacles.

Speaking of insulation, the garage is starting to look pretty scary! The floors and ceilings are going to be R-25, the 6" walls of the live/tracking room are going to be R-21, and the thinner 4" wall are going to be R-15.

That's a rather daunting pile of 2 X 6's that will make up the live/tracking room walls. On the left is about 1200 pounds of Pergo flooring. You should have seen me try to move that mountain on the cart at Costco, it did not even want to roll! 

This mountain of MDF & soundboard (below) is destined for the walls and ceiling of the studio.  

As you can see the existing duct work was in the way of everything and was going to cause me grief. See how it passes under the glue-laminated beam? It was going to be a problem in both rooms. 

So, we changed the HVAC ducts to insulated flexible tubing. (modeled below) We actually fixed a problem with the HVAC system and now the bonus room and the kitchen are getting heated and cooled better than ever before since the volume of air has been double to these rooms. 

Some of the flex ducts were stuffed into the overhead space in-between the 2" X 12" roof joists, and some of them when over the beam. It's hard to tell from the picture below, but al of the big white ducts have been removed which freed up a lot of space in the room. 

Well, the floor is in place now, both floor are finally at the same level. It is three layers: a 3/4" plywood base, 1/2" sound board, 1/2" high density MDF. We screwed down the MDF with cabinetry screws, it's not going anywhere! Ultimately it will have 1/4" maple Pergo over the MDF.

I got a new toy today that's going to pay for itself rather quickly; it's a laser square. I just used it when I drilled and bolted  the  2 X 6 base plates onto the foundation to square it up. It projects perfect XYZ axes. Pretty cool! You can't tell from the picture below, but it's also shooting a beam straight up.

I spent the entire weekend under the crawlspace. <yuck>  When I emerged I looked like chimney sweep! 

I figured out why the floor would bounce & squeak, the previous owner had never properly attached the beams to the 4 X 4 posts, or the posts to the cement pads. After a few hours of work the floor was nice and solid.  

I added the all-important vapor barrier because, believe it, or not, there wasn't one in place.

I stuck 9" of insulation in-between the joists of the existing floor.

Then, I suspended the remaining flexible HVAC duct from the joists with fiberglass strapping. Moving this duct from ceiling of the room above to this under-floor configuration freed up a lot of real-estate in the room.

I spent about 10 hours under the floor in the crawlspace last weekend (yuck), and three hours this weekend,  tapping into the house HVAC system for my two new rooms. I don't have any pictures and I don't feel like going back down there any time soon. 

I had full access to the 10" supply duct under the floor, so I just cut the duct and inserted a "Y" which I then inserted a another "Y"' to feed the two rooms. 

There was no return air access, so I had to make my own. I had to punch through the wall into the furnace room (below) and then tap into the side of the main return air duct.

Because I am concerned about sound from the rooms leaking back into the house HVAC & visa-versa, I inserted a home brew silencers on each of the feeds in and out of the room. I took duct elbows and made "S" shaped ducts that had no line of site and 4 turns to them. Hopefully this will help attenuate any sound that leaks into the ducts. 

If you're wondering why the return air vent is so small, 6", well, what I'm actually doing is connecting what was originally the 4" duct  fresh-air exchange system into the house HVAC due to budget limitations. So, this is actually the right size. The studio was going to have a closed system A/C of its own, but at this point that's outside the scope of my budget.. If leakage turns out to be an unbearable problem (hopefully not) I have full access to the ducts under the floor and can disconnect it from the house HVAC and put it onto its own system. 

Anyway, when I fired up the HVAC everything worked the first time! The input vents in each room were blowing air into the room, and the output vents were pulling air out of the room. I also noticed that there wasn't any noise coming from any of the vents, so perhaps my attenuation scheme, and all of the other turns in the ducts took their toll on the furnace noise. Yes!

I also stopped using paper binders for my cheat sheets; gone are the clothes pins used to keep sheets from blowing away or pages from turning. I have a new 24" Sony VAIO all-in-winder touchpad PC.  I built a stand for it that holds all of my gear pre-wired and ready to rock and roll - literally.  It only needs power and has outputs for my guitar DSP and vocals. The bands WiFi router is in this rig too, when connected to the new Presonus Mixer we can all connect and change our unique monitor mixes via iPads.  We can even set up for a gig, play a few tunes, then put our instruments down and walk out in front with the iPad and fine tune the FOH mix. Pretty cool!  The computer streams each channel back through the mixer individually as if we were playing.

If you look above you can see foot pedals, I wired them to a USB interface and I use them to turn song pages as I'm singing. I used Adobe Acrobat Pro to create PDF "binders" which turned out to be a really slick solution. I can turn pages and switch songs in the binder. As BSOD insurance I still bring paper copies though, better safe that sorry.

The stand also includes a four way HDMI splitter, so as we are learning songs we can all see the lyrics/chords, it also comes in handy live to jog everyone's memory as we count in. It's extremely nice when someone asks for a song we haven't played in forever and a day and did not practice leading up to a gig.

 

Want to hear a funny story? The touchscreen worked flawlessly until we had a night gig by a swimming pool. The water and the lights attracted bugs which were landing on my screen during songs.  What a nightmare! Windows faithfully registered multi-touch events and as the bugs walked owards each other everything shrank too small to be readable and as they walked away from each other the font was insanely large. I even witnessed a few select and copy events in Adobe. At the time it wasn't so funny ... I was stressed but still had a sense of humor, I remember thinking, "Use the FARCE Eric, use the FARCE" and somewhere in the way back of my mind the chords and lyrics were actually there all along.  Needless to say I have since figured out how to disable all touch features for outside gigs. 

We have also added some DMX LED lighting  to the fray, my son Ryan (age 11)  does all of the lighting and set up the software for us on my wife Karen's laptop. They are hanging on my PVC tubing transport rack, I came up with that solution after they almost self destructed in transport to their first gig, they look solid but are actually fragile. Someday I'll go wireless, oh yes ... oh yes,

When Ryan is into it he'll sit there and interactively switch between patches he programmed which is awesome, and when he is not he'll leave it on autopilot. He blew his 5th grade teachers away this year when he offered to provide a background light show for their 2001 a Space Odyssey based performance. 

It sure beats our previous christmas tree lights based solution!

Also new is my custom "Power Snake" which is the glue that binds everything together.  All audio and power cables are good to go, shrink tubed, in braided sleeving, and labeled.

We just dump it out and it fans into and "H" pattern and everyone plugs in. Even HDMI video monitors and powered audio monitors have signal and power for each player. This is the result of a very long "lost weekend". :-) I have to say, it was worth every minute invested. Our setup time used to be a confusing 90 minutes or more where we strugged to remember how things were hooked up last time, and now we can be playing in about 20 minutes if we hustle.

I also added a Dell docking bay connected to all three wrap-around screens and a KVM so I can connect my laptop if need be.
